What is HCM and How does it affect the Heart?
Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM) is a genetic heart disorder which creates thickened walls of the heart.
HCM can cause several different problems in the heart including:
OBSTRUCTION
Blood has difficulty leaving the left ventricle (the main pumping chamber of the heart).
STIFFENING
Increased stiffening causes difficulty in the heart's ability to relax.
ATRIAL FIBRILLATION
An abnormal heart rhythm in the upper chambers.
SUDDEN DEATH
An abnormal heart rhythm in the lower chambers of the heart causing a failure of blood flow through the body.
ADVANCED HEART FAILURE
The heart muscle becomes too fatigued to function normally leading to early death or heart transplantation.
What are the symptoms and risk factors?
HCM can cause symptoms ranging from mild to severe, including palpitations, shortness of breath, chest pain, dizziness, lightheadedness, and fatigue. It is advisable to seek a consultation with a cardiologist knowledgeable in HCM for evaluation.
When does it develop?
HCM can develop at any age but most commonly develops in association with growth and is usually apparent by the late teens or early twenties.
What are the risk factors?
Those with a family member with the disease have an increased risk. If the parent has the disease, they have a 50/50 chance.
How is it diagnosed?
HCM is diagnosed primarily with imaging of the heart with an echocardiogram and in some cases a cardiac MRI. Genetic testing is used for those with a family member who has been clinically diagnosed.
